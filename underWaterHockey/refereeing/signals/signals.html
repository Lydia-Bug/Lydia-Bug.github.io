Signals are used to signal the other refs, and players. Signals need to be clear and above water. Before signaling an infringement you should signal stop play. To find a particular signal use ctrl F.

Ready to Start Play
One arm raised in the air with signal hand open and fingers together.

This is used at the start the game, or after a goal is scored when both teams are on their side of the pool, to signal to the other referee that you are ready to start the game.

This is also used to signal you are ready to start an advantage or equal puck, along with the other arm creating a vertical line.

Stop Play
One arm extended into the air, with the signal hand open. Giving a wide rapid waving motion side to side.

This is used to signal that you are stopping play in order to signal an infringement.

Time
One hand on top of the other hand to make a ‘T’ sign.

This is used to signal to players that either the first half, or whole game has ended.

Equal Puck
Both arms are crossed above the head to form a ‘X’.

This is used when an infringement has occurred, or the game play had to be stopped, and no particular team is at fault.

For more information about what constitutes for an equal puck, and how to set it up, click here.

Time Penalty
One arm points to the infringing player, and then points to the penalty box. The other hand shows the penalty time, by show one, one, two, or five fingers.

Time penalties are usually used when an infringement is repeated, on purpose, or dangerous.

To learn more about what constitutes a time penalty and the time penalty they should get, click here.

Total Dismissal
One arm points at the dismissed player, and one arm swings back and forth horizontally with the water making a 90° arc. Then points to the penalty box.

Players are usually dismissed after repeatedly playing dangerously, majorly affecting the play of the game, and insulting referees and players. To learn more about what would constitute for total dismissal click here.

Goal
Both arms raised straight into the air with hands open.

This is to signal that a goal has been scored.

Before signaling a goal, you should check with the other referees in the pool, by doing ‘finger guns’ at them, if both referees doing ‘finger guns’ back then you can signal a goal. If not then talk with referees first to decide if it's a goal or no-goal.

No Goal or Goal Successfully Defended (Penalty Shot)
Both arms make simultaneous arcs while perpendicular to the water.

A no goal is signaled, there was a possibility of a goal and after the referees agreed it wasn’t a goal, or after a penalty shot was successfully defended.

Penalty Shot/Goal
Vertically pumping one arm up and down from the shoulder with a clenched fist, or doing the ‘cho cho’ motion.

If the motion is done before signaling goal, then a penalty goal is awarded. If the signal is done by itself it means a penalty shot is awarded. You should discuss with your other referees before signaling a penalty shot or goal.

To learn more about when to award a penalty shot or goal, and how they work click here.

Illegal use of Free Arm
One arm is parallel to the water with a clenched fist. With a bent elbow moves horizontally in a pumping motion.

This is signaled when a player uses their free arm to block other players, and to shield the puck.

Obstruction, Barging, Blocking, Shapharding
Two arms parallel from the water held together, moving back and forth from the chest.

This is called when you use your body to block, or barge into a player.

Advancing the puck with Glove, Free Hand, or body
One hand is open, while the other punches it with a clenched fist.

This is called when the puck is moved forward or outhed by anything other then the playing area of the stick. This is typically called for gloving; when the puck touches glove.

Illegally Stopping the Puck
An open hand is moved up and down in a chopping motion

This is called when you stop the puck with your hand, or any where that isn’t the playing area of the puck

Stick Infringement
One arm is held parallel to the chest and water, then other hand makes a repeated chopping motion on top of the other arm.

This is called when you push, pull, or hit the other player stick with your own stick.

Illegal Substitution
Hand over hand in a rolling motion.

This is called when a subing player gets into the pool before the other player is fully out. Or if there a are players in the pool then there should be; there should be no more than six players minus the amount of the players that are in the goal bin.

Unsportsmanlike Conduct (headshots)
With clenched fist repeatedly tap on the side of your head.

This is called most commonly for headshots, or flicks that go close to the head, but can also be called for fighting, swearing and removing equipment.

Breaking, False Start, or Encroachment
One arm is raised with index finger pointing up, rotated several times, tracing an imaginary circle.

This is called if someone is not touching the wall before, before the start of play. Or if one team crosses the line, or goes down before they are appost to, at an advantage or equal puck.

Grabbing and/or pulling on Wall Barriers or Gullies
One arm is raised with index finger pointing up, rotated several times, tracing an imaginary circle.

This is called if someone is not touching the wall before, before the start of play. Or if one team crosses the line, or goes down before they are appost to, at an advantage or equal puck.

Infringement Seen but the Advantage rule is in effect
This signal is done under the water, the index finger is pointed and moved in a circular motion.

This is done if you have seen an infringement, but it didn’t affected the team that was being infringed on, so there is no need to penalises the infringing team.

Side-line-out or Puck-out-of-bounds
A straight forearm is held above the water. The other hand makes a diving motion over the arm, mimicking a puck being flicked over the barrier.

This signal is called when the puck leaves the playing area. Either when the puck has been pushed under the barriers, or been flicked other them.

Delay of game (corner rule)
One hand is on opposite shoulder and elbow points forward.

This is called when a team isn’t trying to further the puck up the pool or get a goal, but is simply trying to make such the other team doesn’t gain possession of it. This can happen anywhere in the pool, but almost always happens in the corner (corner rule) because it’s easy to keep it there.
